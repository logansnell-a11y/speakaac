# Speak AAC: A Private Safety Reporting Channel for Nonverbal Children
## One-Page Research Concept — NIDILRR Field-Initiated Projects

---

### Problem

Nonverbal and minimally verbal children face a well-documented 3–4 times greater risk of abuse than their speaking peers, yet have no effective mechanism to report it. Existing augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) tools provide no pathway for a child to disclose abuse or signal distress to a trusted adult outside the primary caretaker relationship. Speech-language pathologists, educators, and clinicians working with this population lack a standardized, technology-supported method for detecting or facilitating disclosures from nonverbal users.

---

### Technology

Speak is a free, open-access AAC application for nonverbal and minimally verbal children that includes two features absent from existing commercial tools:

**1. Diagnosis-personalized onboarding.** The app automatically configures the symbol grid, vocabulary complexity, motor targeting, and visual layout based on the child's diagnosis, age range, motor ability, and sensory profile — across 17 supported conditions including autism spectrum disorder, Down syndrome, cerebral palsy, apraxia, and acquired brain injury. This eliminates the months-long manual customization process that typically precedes AAC deployment.

**2. Private safety reporting channel.** A child can silently alert a designated trusted adult (outside the primary caretaker relationship) by activating a hidden symbol sequence or a dedicated Help button. The alert fires an immediate, silent email to the trusted contact — no visible action on the device, no mail app opened, no caretaker awareness. This is the first feature of its kind in any publicly available AAC system.

The app is live, installable on iPads and Android tablets, and accessible at speakaac.org.

---

### Research Questions

1. Does diagnosis-personalized AAC configuration reduce time-to-functional-communication compared to standard manual setup?
2. Does access to a private safety reporting channel increase the rate of abuse disclosures among nonverbal children in clinical and school settings over a 6-month period?
3. What barriers exist to reliable use of a private safety channel by nonverbal children, and how can the interface be modified to reduce them?

---

### Proposed Design

A 6-month pilot study with 30–50 nonverbal children (ages 4–16) across clinical and school-based settings. Participants would be randomized to Speak with the safety channel active vs. Speak with the safety channel inactive. Outcome measures would include: number of safety alerts generated, response time from trusted contacts, caretaker-reported communication milestones, SLP-reported ease of setup, and structured disclosure interviews at 3 and 6 months.

All participants would receive a functional AAC tool regardless of group assignment. IRB oversight, participant recruitment, clinical assessment, and data analysis would be provided by the university research partner.
